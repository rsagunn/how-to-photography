Exposure Triangle

The exposure triangle is a fundamental concept in photography that illustrates the relationship between three key elements: aperture, shutter speed, and ISO. Together, these elements control the exposure, or the amount of light that reaches the camera sensor. Here's what each element is:

  1. Aperature

    This refers to the size of the opening in the lens through which light passes. It's measured in f-stops (e.g., f/2.8, f/4, f/8). A lower f-stop number means a larger aperture, allowing more light in and creating a shallow depth of field (blurry background). A higher f-stop number means a smaller aperture, allowing less light in and creating a greater depth of field (more of the scene in focus).

  2. Shutter speed

    This is the length of time the camera's shutter is open, allowing light to hit the sensor. It's measured in seconds or fractions of a second (e.g., 1/250, 1/60, 1", 30"). A faster shutter speed (e.g., 1/1000) freezes motion but lets in less light. A slower shutter speed (e.g., 1/30) allows more light in but can result in motion blur if the subject or camera moves.

  3. ISO

    This measures the camera sensor's sensitivity to light. A lower ISO number (e.g., 100, 200) means lower sensitivity, resulting in less noise (grain) but requiring more light. A higher ISO number (e.g., 1600, 3200) increases sensitivity, allowing for shooting in low-light conditions but introducing more noise.
